1 Cartesian coordinates

A point is written as an ordered pair $(x,\,y)$: the x-coordinate first (across), then the y-coordinate (up/down). They are measured from the origin $(0,0)$ where the axes cross.

x-axis — the horizontal line. Right is positive, left is negative.
y-axis — the vertical line. Up is positive, down is negative.
✎
Remember the order: "along the corridor, then up the stairs" — x before y, always.

2 Midpoint & distance between two points

For two points $A(x_1,y_1)$ and $B(x_2,y_2)$:

Midpoint $=\left(\dfrac{x_1+x_2}{2},\ \dfrac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$
Length $AB=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}$
Worked example
For $A(2,3)$ and $B(8,11)$:
Midpoint $=\left(\frac{2+8}{2},\frac{3+11}{2}\right)=(5,7)$.
Length $=\sqrt{(8-2)^2+(11-3)^2}=\sqrt{36+64}=\sqrt{100}=10$.

3 Drawing a straight line from its equation

Every straight line can be written as $y=mx+c$. To draw it, build a small table of values, plot the points, and join them with a ruler.

Worked example — draw $y=2x-1$
x−1012
y−3−113
Plot $(-1,-3),(0,-1),(1,1),(2,3)$ and draw one straight line through them.
✎
Special lines: $x=a$ is a vertical line; $y=b$ is a horizontal line; $y=x$ is the diagonal through the origin at 45°.
  • The y-intercept is where the line crosses the y-axis — set $x=0$.
  • The x-intercept is where the line crosses the x-axis — set $y=0$.

4 Gradient & the equation of a line

The gradient $m$ measures steepness — how much $y$ changes for each step in $x$.

$m=\dfrac{\text{change in }y}{\text{change in }x}=\dfrac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}$
Positive gradient → line goes up ↗
Negative gradient → line goes down ↘

In $y=mx+c$, $m$ is the gradient and $c$ is the y-intercept. To find a line's equation you need its gradient and one point.

Point–gradient form: $y-y_1=m(x-x_1)$
Worked example — line through $(3,16)$ and $(8,31)$
Gradient $m=\dfrac{31-16}{8-3}=\dfrac{15}{5}=3$.
Use $(3,16)$: $16=3(3)+c \Rightarrow c=7$.
Equation: $\mathbf{y=3x+7}$.

5 Parallel & perpendicular lines

Parallel lines never meet — they have the same gradient.

$m_1=m_2$

Perpendicular lines meet at 90° — gradients are negative reciprocals.

$m_1\times m_2=-1$
✎
Negative reciprocal trick: flip the fraction and change the sign. If $m_1=\tfrac{2}{3}$ then the perpendicular gradient is $-\tfrac{3}{2}$.
Worked example
Line $L$: $y=4-5x$, so $m_L=-5$.
Perpendicular gradient $=-\dfrac{1}{-5}=\dfrac{1}{5}$.
Through $(0,6)$: $c=6$, so $\mathbf{y=\tfrac{1}{5}x+6}$.

6 Representing inequalities graphically

  • Draw the boundary line (e.g. for $y\le 2x+1$, draw $y=2x+1$).
  • Solid line for $\le$ or $\ge$ (points on the line are included).
  • Dashed line for $<$ or $>$ (points on the line are excluded).
  • Shade the correct side. Test a point such as $(0,0)$: if it makes the inequality true, that side is the required region.
$y > mx+c$ → region above the line  •  $y < mx+c$ → region below the line
Worked example — is $(0,0)$ in $y \le 2x+1$?
Substitute: $0 \le 2(0)+1 = 1$. True, so shade the side containing the origin.
✎
When several inequalities are combined, the required region $R$ is where all shaded conditions overlap. Cambridge often asks you to label this region $R$.
