Everything for non-linear graphs — quadratic and reciprocal curves, roots and turning points, solving equations graphically, tangents, simultaneous equations, travel graphs and feasible regions. Read the notes, then test yourself on the real Paper 2 & Paper 4 questions below.

1 Recognising non-linear graphs

Quadratic $y=ax^2+bx+c$ — a U-shaped parabola. Opens up if $a>0$, down if $a<0$. One line of symmetry.
Cubic $y=ax^3+\dots$ — an S-shaped curve. Rises left-to-right if $a>0$. Up to two turning points.
Reciprocal $y=\dfrac{a}{x}$ — two branches (a hyperbola) that never touch the axes. The axes are asymptotes.
Exponential $y=a^{x}$ — grows or decays faster and faster; passes through $(0,1)$.
✎
Count the highest power. Power 1 → straight line · power 2 → parabola · power 3 → cubic · $x$ on the bottom → reciprocal.

2 Roots, intercepts, turning points & asymptotes

  • y-intercept — where the curve crosses the y-axis. Put $x=0$ into the equation.
  • x-intercepts / roots — where the curve crosses the x-axis. These are the solutions of $y=0$.
  • Turning point (vertex) — the maximum or minimum where the curve changes direction.
  • Line of symmetry — a vertical line through the turning point of a parabola.
  • Asymptote — a line the curve gets ever closer to but never reaches.
Worked example — $y=x^2-2x-3$
y-intercept: $x=0\Rightarrow y=-3$, so $(0,-3)$.
Roots: $x^2-2x-3=(x-3)(x+1)=0\Rightarrow x=3$ or $x=-1$.
Line of symmetry halfway between the roots: $x=\dfrac{3+(-1)}{2}=1$.
Turning point: $x=1\Rightarrow y=1-2-3=-4$, a minimum at $(1,-4)$.

3 Plotting curves & reading a graph

  • Complete the table of values — substitute each x carefully (watch signs when squaring/cubing negatives).
  • Plot every point; join them with a smooth freehand curve, not straight ruler segments.
  • A common lost mark: drawing a flat bottom on a parabola instead of a single lowest point.
Worked example — complete the table for $y=x^2-3$
x−3−2−10123
y61−2−3−216
The y-values are symmetric — a quick check you have substituted correctly.
✎
Reading values: to find $y$ for a given $x$, go up to the curve then across. To solve for $x$, go across from the y-value to the curve then down.

4 Solving equations graphically

The solutions of $f(x)=0$ are the x-coordinates where the curve crosses the x-axis. The solutions of $f(x)=g(x)$ are where the two graphs intersect.

To solve $f(x)=k$: draw the horizontal line $y=k$ and read off the x-values where it cuts the curve.

If a question gives a drawn curve and asks you to solve a different equation, rearrange so one side is exactly the drawn curve. Whatever is left over is the straight line you must draw.

Worked example
Graph drawn: $y=x^3-2x$. Solve $x^3-2x-1=0$.
Rearrange: $x^3-2x=1$. So draw the line $\mathbf{y=1}$ and read the x-values where it meets the curve.
Harder — solve $x^3-4x+2=0$: rearrange to $x^3-2x=2x-2$, so draw $\mathbf{y=2x-2}$.

5 Gradient of a curve — tangents & rates

  • Draw a tangent — a straight line just touching the curve at the required point.
  • Pick two clear points on the tangent, as far apart as possible for accuracy.
  • Find its gradient with $\dfrac{\text{change in }y}{\text{change in }x}$.
Gradient at a point $=\dfrac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}$ measured along the tangent
✎
The gradient of a curve is a rate of change — on a distance–time graph it is speed; on a speed–time graph it is acceleration.

6 Simultaneous linear & non-linear equations

The solution of a linear and a non-linear equation together is the set of points where their graphs cross. A line can cut a curve at two points, one point (a tangent), or not at all.

Worked example — solve $y=x^2$ and $y=x+2$
Set equal: $x^2=x+2\Rightarrow x^2-x-2=0\Rightarrow (x-2)(x+1)=0$.
So $x=2$ (then $y=4$) or $x=-1$ (then $y=1$).
Intersection points: $(2,4)$ and $(-1,1)$.
✎
Method: substitute the linear equation into the curve, collect everything on one side to get a quadratic $=0$, then solve. Always find both x and y for each point.

7 Travel graphs — distance–time & speed–time

Distance–time graphs

  • Gradient = speed. Steeper means faster.
  • A horizontal line means stationary.
  • A curve means the speed is changing.

Speed–time graphs

  • Gradient = acceleration. Negative gradient is deceleration.
  • Area under the graph = distance travelled.
  • A horizontal line means constant speed.
acceleration $=\dfrac{\text{change in speed}}{\text{time}}\qquad$ distance $=$ area under a speed–time graph
Worked example — area of a trapezium
A car speeds up from 0 to 12 m/s in 30 s, then holds 12 m/s for 60 s.
Distance = triangle + rectangle $=\tfrac12(30)(12)+(60)(12)=180+720=900$ m.

8 Feasible regions — linear inequalities

  • For each inequality, draw the boundary line (replace the inequality sign with $=$).
  • Solid line for $\le$ or $\ge$; dashed line for $<$ or $>$.
  • Decide which side to keep by testing a point such as $(0,0)$.
  • The feasible region $R$ is where all the required sides overlap.
$y > mx+c$ → above the line  •  $y < mx+c$ → below  •  $x>a$ → right of a vertical line
✎
Exam tip: Cambridge usually asks you to label the region $R$. Many candidates shade the unwanted side — always test a point to be sure.
